# SECTION: PREPARED_REMARKS_CEO
section_id: investor_ceo_remarks

## CEO — CEO Prepared Remarks

Thank you, Martin. Q1 2030 was a landmark quarter for Tesla.

**The Trillion-Dollar Milestone.**
On January 22nd, Tesla became the sixth company in history to cross one trillion dollars in annualized revenue. As of today, our run-rate is $1.2 trillion. What makes this different from other trillion-dollar companies is the speed — we did it in 21 years from founding. And unlike Walmart or Amazon, our revenue is diversified across six distinct business lines, each of which could be a standalone Fortune 500 company.

**Vehicle Business.**
We delivered 1.2 million vehicles in Q1, putting us on track for 4.8 million this year. The Model 2 launched in February and immediately became our fastest-selling model ever. Production is ramping at Shanghai and Berlin — we expect to hit 200,000 Model 2 units per month by Q3. Fremont is now exclusively producing premium vehicles — Model S, X, and Cybertruck — operating at a 38% gross margin.

**Robotaxi.**
This is the story of the quarter. Robotaxi revenue hit $847 million per day — that's a $309 billion annualized run-rate. We're operating in 340 cities across 28 countries. The unit economics are extraordinary: 64% gross margin, $0.18 per mile total cost vs. $0.94 for rideshare competitors. We added 42 new cities in Q1 alone, and we're targeting 500 cities by year-end.

The EU approved L4 urban driving in 18 countries in January, which was the unlock for European robotaxi expansion. We went from 12 European cities to 48 in one quarter.

**Optimus.**
42,000 Optimus units are now deployed — 38,800 in industrial settings and 3,200 in consumer pre-orders for the Home Edition launching May 15th. Revenue is at $120 billion annualized, up 328% year-over-year. The unit cost dropped to $9,200 from $14,800 a year ago, which is what enables the $16,000 consumer price point.

I want to be direct: I believe Optimus will eventually be bigger than our vehicle business. The total addressable market for humanoid robots is north of $30 trillion. We're in the first inning.

**Dojo.**
4.7 exaFLOPS of total compute across 4 clusters. We had the Jakarta outage on March 8th — cooling relay failure, 12% capacity loss for about 6 hours. Singapore failover handled it. Full recovery by March 16th. The CaaS business is at $36 billion annualized, and our customer pipeline includes 14 enterprise clients, three of which are Fortune 100. We cut pricing by 15% in March to undercut AWS and Google — expect volume to more than offset the price reduction by Q4.

**Energy.**
$74 billion annualized. Megapack deployments up 42% year-over-year. The Pilbara installation in Western Australia — our largest single project ever at 4.2 GWh — came online in February. We're now the world's largest energy storage provider by deployed capacity.

**What I'm Focused On.**
Three things: Model 2 ramp speed, Optimus Home Edition quality at launch, and Dojo v3 chip tape-out in Q3. These three programs will define whether we're a $1.5 trillion or $2 trillion revenue company by the end of 2031.

# SECTION: PREPARED_REMARKS_CFO
section_id: investor_cfo_remarks

## Vaibhav Taneja — CFO Prepared Remarks

Thank you, you. I'll walk through the financials.

**Revenue.**
Q1 2030 revenue came in at $310 billion, up 28.1% year-over-year and 6.2% sequentially. This beat consensus estimates by $12 billion. All six divisions grew, with Robotaxi and Optimus leading.

Revenue by division:
- Vehicle Sales: $120B (quarterly) / $480B annualized — up 17.6% YoY
- Robotaxi: $77.3B / $309B annualized — up 79.7% YoY
- Energy: $18.5B / $74B annualized — up 42.3% YoY
- Optimus: $30B / $120B annualized — up 328% YoY
- Software & Services: $18B / $72B annualized — up 50% YoY
- Dojo CaaS: $9B / $36B annualized — up 157% YoY
- Licensing & Other: $8.9B / $35.8B annualized

**Margins.**
Blended gross margin expanded to 31.3%, up from 28.1% a year ago. The big drivers: Robotaxi at 64%, Software at 75%, and Optimus improving to 42% as we scale production. Vehicle margins remained stable at 25% despite the Model 2 launch at a lower ASP — manufacturing efficiency gains at Mumbai and Shanghai offset the price mix.

Operating margin: 24.0%, up from 20.1% a year ago. This is the highest operating margin we've ever achieved as a company.

**Earnings.**
Net income: $60 billion (projected full quarter). Diluted EPS: $17.80, up 59% year-over-year. We continue to see significant operating leverage.

**Cash & Capital.**
We ended Q1 with $142 billion in cash and equivalents, plus $28 billion in short-term investments — total liquidity of $170 billion. Free cash flow for the quarter was $21.5 billion, representing a 6.9% FCF margin.

Total debt: $28 billion, down $4 billion from Q4. We paid down $4 billion in senior notes that matured in February.

**CapEx.**
Total 2030 CapEx budget is $48 billion. We've deployed $11.1 billion year-to-date, tracking to plan. The largest allocation is $12 billion for Dojo expansion, followed by $18 billion for manufacturing.

The Beijing Giga pre-construction allocation of $3.8 billion was approved by our board in March. Environmental review is underway.

**Effective Tax Rate.**
14.2%, down from 16.8% in 2029, driven by R&D credits and our international tax structure. US IRA manufacturing credits contributed $4.8 billion in annual value.

I'll now hand it back to Martin for Q&A.

# SECTION: QA_SESSION
section_id: investor_qa

## Q&A Session

### Q1: Tasha Keeney — ARK Invest
**Tasha:** you, Optimus Home Edition — the $16,000 price point is aggressive. Can you walk us through the unit economics and when you expect the consumer segment to reach gross margin parity with industrial?

**You:** Great question. The $16,000 price point is enabled by Gen-3 actuators, which are 40% cheaper than Gen-2. BOM cost is currently $9,200, so we're at about 42% gross margin on the industrial units. The Home Edition has additional software and safety costs — call it $11,400 all-in — so gross margin is around 29% at launch. We expect to reach 40%+ by Q4 2030 as we scale past 10,000 consumer units per month. The lease at $299/month is actually higher margin due to software subscription bundling.

**Tasha:** And the TAM?

**You:** ARK's own modeling says $2.4 trillion by 2035. I think that's conservative. Every household that can afford a dishwasher can eventually afford an Optimus. That's 500 million homes globally.

### Q2: Adam Jonas — Morgan Stanley
**Adam:** Vaibhav, the operating margin expansion is impressive. How sustainable is 24%? Where does it plateau?

**Vaibhav:** Adam, the margin expansion is structural, not cyclical. Three factors: Robotaxi is a 64% gross margin business that's growing faster than vehicles. Software is 75% margin and expanding. And manufacturing cost per vehicle continues to decline — we're at $18,400 blended, down 13% YoY. I'm confident we can sustain above 24% for the foreseeable future. The ceiling is probably 28-30% as Robotaxi and Optimus become larger shares of the mix.

**Adam:** And on the buyback question that keeps coming up?

**Vaibhav:** We still believe reinvesting in growth generates better returns than buybacks. With $142 billion in net cash and $48 billion in CapEx opportunities, we're deploying capital at 30%+ ROIC. A buyback at a $6 trillion market cap would be symbolic, not material. You?

**You:** Yeah, I'd rather build robots than buy back stock. We'll revisit when growth moderates, which I don't see happening before 2032.

### Q3: Mark Delaney — Goldman Sachs
**Mark:** Tom, can you give us more detail on the Jakarta Dojo outage? What's the financial exposure from future incidents, and how are you hardening the infrastructure?

**Tom Zhu:** The Jakarta outage was a single cooling relay failure — a $400 part that cascaded into a 12% capacity loss for 6 hours. Total financial impact was approximately $2.4 million in delayed training and $800K in emergency failover costs. Singapore's cluster absorbed 96% of the workload within 15 minutes, which validated our geographic redundancy architecture.

Going forward: we're implementing predictive relay monitoring across all 4 clusters — 14,000 relays total. The system will flag degradation 72 hours before failure. That retrofit costs $18 million and will be complete by May.

**Mark:** And the CaaS impact?

**Tom:** Zero client-facing impact. All CaaS workloads are SLA-guaranteed with automatic failover. No client credits were issued.

### Q4: Ryan Brinkman — JP Morgan
**Ryan:** you, on Model 2 — the EU pricing at €24,900 is below your original €28,000 plan. How does that square with the margin targets? Are you buying market share?

**You:** Yes, deliberately. BYD doesn't have a European factory until 2032. We have a 24-month window where we're the only sub-€25,000 EV with genuine autonomy capability. The margin compression is real — 18.4% gross margin on EU Model 2 vs. 25% blended — but the volume forecast is 800,000 units by Q4. At that volume, the margin math works. And every Model 2 sold is a future robotaxi, FSD subscriber, and energy customer.

**Ryan:** And US Model 2 pricing?

**You:** $22,490 before federal credit. After the $7,500 IRA credit, it's $14,990. At that price, we're replacing the used car market.

### Q5: Colin Rusch — Oppenheimer
**Colin:** On Dojo CaaS, you cut pricing 15%. How do you compete against hyperscalers who are also cutting prices aggressively?

**You:** We have a structural advantage they don't: vertical integration. We design the chips, build the cooling systems, and own the real estate. Our cost per TFLOP is $0.14 — that's 62% below AWS Trainium at $0.37. Even at the new $0.357 per TFLOP-hour pricing, our gross margin on CaaS is 61%. We can go lower if we need to. And our inference latency is 40% better because of the custom interconnect architecture.

**Colin:** Client concentration risk?

**Ashok Elluswamy:** No single client exceeds 8% of CaaS revenue. We have 14 enterprise clients in active negotiation, and our existing 38 clients span automotive, pharma, finance, and government sectors.

### Q6: Emmanuel Rosner — Deutsche Bank
**Emmanuel:** On the Beijing Giga — 14-month approval timeline. What's your contingency if China throws up regulatory obstacles?

**You:** We have a contingency, but I don't think we'll need it. Our Shanghai Giga has been the most productive factory in China for six consecutive years. The Chinese government wants us there — we employ 18,000 people in Shanghai and our supply chain supports 40,000 additional jobs. Beijing Giga is a $12 billion investment. Governments don't block $12 billion investments from companies with clean track records.

**Vaibhav:** On the financial side, if Beijing is delayed by 6 months, the CapEx simply shifts to Q1 2031. There's no impairment or write-down risk.

### Q7: Alexander Potter — Piper Sandler
**Alex:** you, your board discussed CEO time allocation. Can you give investors confidence that Tesla has your full attention?

**You:** [Laughs] Tesla is the most important thing I do. SpaceX runs itself at this point — Gwynne has been CEO-in-all-but-title for years. xAI has a separate team. My commitment to the board is a minimum of 60% of my working time to Tesla — and given that my working time is about 100 hours a week, that's 60 hours on Tesla. That's more than most CEOs put in total. Tom Zhu and I talk 4-5 times a day. I approve every major product decision. Tesla has my attention.

### Q8: Dan Levy — Barclays
**Dan:** Vaibhav, on the free cash flow — $86 billion annualized. At what point does this become a capital allocation problem? Investors are going to start asking harder questions about the cash pile.

**Vaibhav:** Dan, I understand the concern, but let me frame it differently. We have $48 billion in CapEx this year. We're evaluating a $12 billion Beijing Giga. Dojo v3 is a $15 billion multi-year program. Optimus consumer launch requires $8 billion in working capital. And we're maintaining a $50 billion strategic reserve for M&A opportunities — You have been clear that there are potential acquisitions in the battery supply chain and robotics components space. We are not hoarding cash; we're warehousing it for deployment.

**You:** I'll add — we will do acquisitions this year. Not massive ones, but strategic ones. Battery technology companies, sensor manufacturers, maybe a materials company. I'm looking at 3-4 targets actively.

## CEO — Closing Remarks

Let me close with some perspective. Five years ago, in 2025, Tesla was a $95 billion revenue company trying to prove that EVs could be profitable. Today, we're a $1.2 trillion revenue company with six distinct business lines, each growing at 20%+ annually. We have more cash than we can spend responsibly, the best AI team in the world, and products that people genuinely love.

But I want to be clear — we are not done. We are not even close. Optimus alone has the potential to generate more revenue than our entire current business. Energy storage will power the transition away from fossil fuels. And autonomous transport will reshape cities in ways we haven't imagined.

The mission hasn't changed: accelerate the world's transition to sustainable energy. What's changed is that we now have the scale, the capital, and the technology to actually do it.

Thank you for your continued support. We'll see you next quarter.
